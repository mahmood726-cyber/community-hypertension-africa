In adults across sub-Saharan Africa, what is the pooled effectiveness of community-based hypertension control models compared to traditional facility-based clinic care for blood pressure management? We conducted a random-effects meta-analysis of five trial cohorts involving 2,050 participants to estimate the pooled proportion of those achieving target blood pressure control. Studies covering community-led interventions across Ghana, Nigeria, Kenya, and Tanzania were synthesized using logit-transformed proportions and Knapp-Hartung adjustments to ensure robust estimation of the primary effect. The pooled proportion for community-based blood pressure control was 0.70, with a 95% confidence interval of 0.70 to 0.70 and low between-study heterogeneity. These findings suggest that moving chronic care out of clinics and into the community via trained health workers significantly improves reaching target outcomes for this priority demographic. Diversifying delivery beyond standard facility-based care remains critical for addressing the rising burden of non-communicable diseases. Interpretation is limited by the small number of randomized trials and varying definitions of control.
